🏘 Towns Near the Villa

Figline Valdarno
10 min · Our home base town
  • A proper medieval market town enclosed within ancient walls, centred on Piazza Marsilio Ficino — one of the best-preserved medieval piazzas in the Valdarno
  • Named after philosopher Marsilio Ficino, born here in 1433
  • Tuesday morning market in the main piazza — local produce, cheese, salumi, fresh bread, clothing and general goods (7am–12:30pm)
  • Good cafés, local shops, and a relaxed Italian small-town atmosphere. Easy for morning coffee, grocery runs, or a casual evening out.
Osteria Vinicolo
⭐ 4.9 (194 reviews) · Piazza Bianco Bianchi 6, Figline Valdarno
Cappelletto with truffles · Creative seasonal pasta dishes · Homemade bread
One reviewer says it deserves a Michelin star. Book ahead — fills up fast. Closed Wednesdays. Dinner only Mon/Tue.
Osteria De' Medici
⭐ 4.7 (3,023 reviews) · Piazza Giuseppe Capanni 13, Incisa in Val d'Arno
Bistecca alla Fiorentina · Homemade pasta · Roasted potatoes · Desserts
Reggello + Vallombrosa Abbey
15 min
  • Quiet hilltop comune surrounded by ancient chestnut and pine forests
  • Vallombrosa Abbey — a Benedictine monastery founded in 1038, set deep inside a stunning old-growth forest. The drive up through the forest alone is worth the trip.
  • Also home to the Pieve di Cascia, a small Romanesque church housing one of Masaccio's earliest known paintings
  • Great option for anyone who wants a slower, nature-focused half-day. Suitable for all mobility levels.
Loro Ciuffenna
25 min · One of the most beautiful villages in Italy
  • A compact medieval borgo built into a dramatic rocky gorge above the rushing Ciuffenna river. Stone lanes, arched passageways, medieval houses stacked above the water — very photogenic and completely tourist-free.
  • Home to the oldest working watermill in Tuscany, built around 1100, still grinding chestnut flour today — built directly into the cliff face above the river
  • Porta dell'Orologio gate and Roman bridge at the village entrance · Church of Santa Maria Assunta with 13th and 14th century frescoes
  • Just outside: Pieve di San Pietro a Gropina — a Romanesque church built around 1000, considered one of the finest examples of Romanesque architecture in Tuscany
  • A 2–3 hour visit covers everything comfortably — perfect morning half-day
Val d'Orcia — Pienza + Montepulciano
~1 hr · UNESCO World Heritage landscape
The most iconic Tuscan scenery — rolling golden hills, cypress avenues, and hilltop villages. The drive south from the villa through the valley is one of the most beautiful in Italy.
  • Pienza — Renaissance town designed by Pope Pius II in the 15th century. Famous for Pecorino di Pienza — tastings often free along the main street. Dramatic terrace with sweeping Val d'Orcia views. Very compact and flat — 1.5 hours comfortably.
  • Montepulciano (20 min from Pienza) — Dramatic hilltop town at 605m. Famous for Vino Nobile DOCG. Historic underground wine cellars (Cantine Storiche) dating to the Etruscans — walk in and taste right at the source. Note: the climb is steep — those with mobility considerations may prefer to stay in the lower town.
Arezzo
40 min · ⭐ Highly underrated
The closest significant city to the villa, dramatically undervisited compared to Florence and Siena. Easy full-day visit.
  • Piazza Grande — one of the most beautiful sloping medieval piazzas in Italy. Far less touristy than anything in Florence.
  • Basilica di San Francesco — houses the Legend of the True Cross fresco cycle by Piero della Francesca, considered one of the greatest achievements of Renaissance painting.
  • Beautiful historic centre, good shopping, excellent local restaurants
  • Suits all mobility levels — compact and flat enough for the base tier

☀️ Weather in July

Italy in July is peak summer — hot, dry, and sunny with long days. It stays light until after 9pm, which is great for evening strolls and late dinners. Rainfall is minimal and usually just brief afternoon thunderstorms.

  • Tuscany (Florence, Figline, Siena): expect 88–93°F most days. Florence in particular holds heat — midday can feel significantly hotter than the actual temperature.
  • Portofino and Cinque Terre (coastal Liguria): slightly cooler with sea breezes, but still 82–88°F.
  • Rome (departure night): can exceed 95°F in July. Plan the late-night Rome tour strategically — after midnight it's much more comfortable.
  • Evenings at the villa: pleasant and cooler, especially with the pool. This is your refuge from the midday heat on big day-trip days.

🎒 What to Pack

  • Lightweight fabrics: cotton and linen are your best friends. Avoid synthetics — they trap heat on cobblestone streets in 90°F weather.
  • Sun protection: sunglasses, a wide-brimmed hat, and high-SPF sunscreen. UV levels are strong all day, not just at peak hours.
  • Light layer for evenings and churches: temperatures drop at night and Italian churches require covered shoulders and knees to enter. A light cardigan or scarf covers both situations.
  • Comfortable walking shoes: every historic city in Italy has cobblestone streets. Sneakers or supportive flats. Save the sandals for the villa and Portofino harbor.
  • Reusable water bottle: free public drinking fountains (called Nasoni) are everywhere in Italian cities — clean, cold, and constantly running. Fill up constantly.

🕛 Beat the Heat

The sun is strongest between noon and 5pm. On Florence days, this lines up perfectly with museum visits — Uffizi and Accademia are best done during the hottest part of the day so you're indoors. Plan outdoor sightseeing for morning and late afternoon.

  • Gelato is not optional. It is a hydration strategy.
  • Most restaurants don't open for dinner until 7:30–8pm — plan accordingly and don't show up hungry at 6pm.
  • Midday in small towns like Figline, businesses close for riposo (the Italian afternoon break) from roughly 1–4pm. Don't count on shops being open.

🍽 Restaurant Etiquette

  • Coperto: most restaurants add a small cover charge (€1–3 per person) to the bill automatically. It's normal and not a mistake — it covers bread and the table.
  • Tipping: not expected the way it is in the US. Rounding up or leaving a few euros is appreciated. Do not tip 20%.
  • Pacing: Italian meals move slowly by design. The waiter will not bring your check until you ask — "Il conto, per favore." This is a feature, not a flaw.
  • Water: tap water is safe to drink everywhere in Italy. At restaurants, ask for "acqua del rubinetto" (tap water) or bring your own bottle — sparkling and still mineral water are both charged for.
  • Coffee: cappuccino is a morning drink only. Ordering one after a meal will confuse your waiter. Espresso or macchiato after lunch and dinner.
